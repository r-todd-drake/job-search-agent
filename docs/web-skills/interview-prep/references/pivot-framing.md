# Pivot Framing — Role Mismatch Reserve Moves

Use this reference when building the GAP-PREPARATION section for a role where the candidate's strongest fit is a variation of the advertised role rather than the exact JD scope.

This section covers language patterns for the reserve pivot — the move deployed only if the interviewer surfaces a gap that appears disqualifying. It is never an opening gambit.

---

## When to include a pivot section

Include a reserve pivot section in GAP-PREPARATION when ALL of the following are true:

1. The JD requires a domain credential or technical depth the candidate does not have
2. The candidate has a strong adjacent capability that maps to a variation of the role
3. Prior interview stages have not already resolved this gap
4. A plausible alternative framing exists that the interviewer could accept

Do NOT include a pivot section when:
- The gap is cosmetic (missing a preferred, not required, qualification)
- The candidate is clearly competitive on the core JD requirements
- The alternative role the candidate might fit is already closed or filled
- The stage is panel or final — by this point the gap has been accepted

---

## The capability reframe — primary pivot pattern

This pattern reframes what the role's core function is, rather than asking to be considered for a different job. It is the preferred pivot in most scenarios.

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge the specific gap directly and without hedging — name what you don't have
2. Reframe the role's core function toward what you do bring
3. Anchor to a concrete story that proves the reframe
4. End with a question or open offer — do not close the door yourself

**Template language:**

> "That's a fair read — and I want to be straight with you rather than spend our time on a gap we both already know is there. My depth is in [your actual capability]. If [role title] needs someone who can personally [the specific thing you can't do], I'm not that person. If it needs someone who can [your genuine value in this role] — that's where I operate."

**Example — Vehicle Systems Lead, vehicle physics gap:**

> "That's a fair read — and I want to be straight with you rather than spend our time on a gap we both already know is there. My depth is in the integration layer: I can sit with an operator, translate desired effects into a ConOps, take that ConOps down through requirements to a performance spec, and build the model that shows the trace. If the Vehicle Systems Lead needs someone who can personally own the propulsion trade studies and hull loading cases, I'm not that person. If it needs someone who can own the performance envelope, define what the hull needs to do, and hold the integration argument across vehicle, payload, and mission systems — that's where I operate."

---

## The disclosure preservation pattern

When the candidate has already disclosed a gap to a recruiter or prior interviewer and been advanced, preserve that disclosure language in the reserve pivot. Do not let the candidate walk back from a framing that already worked.

**Template:**

> "I want to make sure we're calibrated — I was upfront with [your recruiter / the hiring manager] about where my depth is. [Restate the disclosure in one sentence.] If your [role title] needs [what they said they don't have], that's worth knowing now. If it's [their genuine value], that's where I operate."

**Example:**

> "I want to make sure we're calibrated — I was upfront with your recruiter about where my depth is. I don't know naval architecture, but I know how to define for the naval architect what the hull needs to be able to do. If your Vehicle Systems Lead needs to be the naval architect, that's worth knowing now. If it's the person who owns the performance envelope and integration argument — that's where I operate."

---

## The org structure question — indirect pivot

When a plausible adjacent role exists in the org but cannot be named directly (e.g., the role is closed, or it would seem presumptuous to name it), use a closing question to plant the concept without making a direct ask.

**Template:**

> "As you think about how the [three/four] leads need to work together — where do you see the biggest integration risk across the seams, and how are you thinking about where [specific function] lives in the structure?"

This question opens a conversation about org design without the candidate appearing to self-select out of the advertised role.

---

## What the pivot is NOT

- It is not asking to be considered for a different job title
- It is not a concession — it reframes, it does not retreat
- It is not used proactively — it is deployed only when the interviewer surfaces the gap as potentially disqualifying
- It is not available if the only adjacent role is already closed — in that case the only move is the capability reframe, not a role-name reference
- It is not used at panel or final stage — the gap has been accepted by then

---

## Delivery guidance

Always include this note when a pivot section appears in a prep package:

> This is a reserve move — deploy it only if the interviewer signals the gap may be disqualifying. Do not raise it proactively. Deliver it flat and matter-of-factly, not apologetically. End with an open frame ("that's where I operate") rather than a question ("does that work for you?"). The question invites a yes/no that closes options; the open frame invites a response that keeps the conversation going.
